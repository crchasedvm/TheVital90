The Vital 90 Days

An Interactive Protocol for NZ Transition Cow Management

This guide translates the comprehensive transition cow report into an actionable, day-by-day plan. Navigate through the key phases—from 60 days pre-calving to 30 days post-calving—to find targeted advice on nutrition, health, and management to ensure a productive and healthy lactation for your herd.

Phase 1: The Far-Off Dry Period (Days -60 to -21)

This initial phase is foundational. The primary goal is to manage Body Condition Score (BCS) and begin preparing the cow's metabolism for the transition ahead. Management decisions here directly impact her health and productivity weeks later at calving and into early lactation.

Key Goals

  • Achieve target Body Condition Score (BCS).
  • Manage energy intake to avoid over-conditioning.
  • Ensure adequate protein and essential trace elements.
  • Begin foundational mineral supplementation, especially Magnesium.

Nutrition & Feeding

Energy intake is critical. Cows at or below target BCS should receive 100% of ME requirements, while those above target should be restricted to 80-90%. Protein needs are around 12% of the diet, typically met by quality NZ pasture.

Phase 2: The Springer Period (Days -21 to Calving)

This is the most intensive pre-calving phase. The focus shifts dramatically to preparing the cow for the immense calcium demand of calving and lactation. This is when specific strategies to prevent milk fever, like DCAD or Zeolite, are implemented.

Key Goals

  • Prevent clinical and subclinical milk fever (hypocalcemia).
  • Maintain feed intake as calving approaches.
  • Ensure rumen adaptation for the post-calving diet.
  • Continue robust Magnesium supplementation (0.4% of DM).

Nutrition & Feeding

Implement your chosen calcium balance strategy. Keep dietary calcium low (unless using a full DCAD program) to stimulate the cow's own mobilization systems. Avoid high-potassium forages. Monitor urine pH if using a DCAD diet.

Phase 3: The Fresh Cow Period (Days 0 to +30)

The cow has calved and is now in a state of negative energy balance. The absolute priority is to maximize Dry Matter Intake (DMI) to support rapidly increasing milk production and minimize health issues. Nutritional support must be aggressive and precise.

Key Goals

  • Maximize Dry Matter Intake (DMI) with high-quality feed.
  • Provide substantial Calcium supplementation immediately.
  • Manage negative energy balance to prevent ketosis.
  • Support rumen health with adequate effective fibre.

Nutrition & Feeding

Fresh cows are the top priority for feed. Provide unrestricted access to the best pasture and supplements. Supplement with 100-300g limeflour/cow/day. Transition to a positive DCAD diet to stimulate appetite. Ensure adequate protein and carbohydrates for milk production.

Priming the System: Pre-Calving (3 Weeks Out) - Detailed

This section provides a deeper dive into the strategies for ensuring adequate magnesium supply and managing the diet's DCAD to "wake up" her internal calcium mobilization systems.

Magnesium: The Calcium Gatekeeper

Magnesium is essential for absorbing calcium from the gut and mobilizing it from bone. Without it, other calcium strategies fail. Aim for 20g of elemental Mg per cow per day.

Pasture Dusting (MgO): Common, but double or triple the rate in wet weather to account for wastage.
Water Troughs (MgCl/MgSO4): Can reduce water palatability at high doses. Best used in combination with other methods.
Mixed Feed (MgO): Effective on feed pads, but account for 5-15% wastage depending on the feeding system.

Warning: High potassium in pasture (common in NZ) interferes with Mg absorption. Avoid effluent paddocks for springers.

Pre-Calving Calcium Strategy: DCAD vs. Synthetic Zeolite

Two primary strategies exist to prime the cow's calcium metabolism before calving. Explore their mechanisms, benefits, and practical considerations below.

DCAD: Activating Calcium Reserves

By feeding a negative Dietary Cation-Anion Difference (DCAD) diet, you induce a mild metabolic acidosis, which primes the cow to mobilize her own calcium reserves efficiently at calving. This proactive approach significantly reduces the risk of clinical and subclinical milk fever.

This "metabolic training" activates the cow's endogenous calcium mobilization system, making her more efficient at drawing calcium from bone reserves and absorbing it from the diet *before* the massive demand of lactation begins.

Practical DCAD Implementation

Optimal negative DCAD ranges are generally **-8 to -12 meq/100g DM**, but avoid going below -15 meq/100g DM to prevent excessive acidification and reduced feed intake. This diet is typically fed for the **last 2-3 weeks (14-21 days)** prior to expected calving, though up to 42 days can also be effective.

Feed Analysis is Crucial:

Thorough chemical analysis of feed ingredients is vital to accurately determine DCAD levels.

Minimize High Potassium Forages:

Avoid or limit effluent-grazed pastures or other high-potassium feeds.

Anionic Supplements:

To achieve a negative DCAD, the diet is adjusted with anionic supplements. Common examples include **magnesium sulphate** and **ammonium chloride**. These compounds provide negatively charged ions that help acidify the cow's blood, thereby stimulating her calcium mobilization system. Commercial anionic pre-calving nuts or pellets are also available in New Zealand, specifically formulated with these salts, along with starch, protein, vitamins, and trace minerals. These are typically fed at a rate of 2kg per cow per day. The choice of anionic salt depends on palatability, cost, and the specific dietary needs of the herd.

Ensure Mineral Balance (Calcium & Phosphorus):

Alongside DCAD adjustment, it is essential to provide adequate levels of other key minerals. Ensure sufficient **magnesium** (>0.40% DM), **calcium**, and **phosphorus**. While the DCAD diet aims to activate calcium mobilization, research shows that feeding more than **180 grams of dietary calcium** with a fully acidogenic diet makes cows more resistant to drops in serum calcium concentrations. This indicates that while restricting calcium *can* prime the system, ensuring sufficient overall calcium *intake* within an acidogenic diet is also beneficial. Phosphorus is crucial for energy metabolism and bone health; its balance with calcium is vital for overall mineral homeostasis.

Monitoring DCAD Effectiveness

Regular monitoring ensures your DCAD program is working as intended. Urine pH is a key indicator.

Urine pH Target (Holsteins):

6.0-6.8. Test at least 3 days after starting the DCAD diet.

Urine pH Target (Jerseys):

5.5-6.0. Account for breed-specific metabolic differences.

Blood Testing:

On day 3 calved cows, blood tests for energy and trace elements provide valuable feedback on transition progress. Note: DCAD diets can potentially harm reproduction in first-calf heifers; consult your nutritionist.

Synthetic Zeolite: Calcium Binding for Activation

Synthetic zeolite (e.g., RECALM) is a calcium binder that can be fed pre-calving to reduce dietary calcium absorption. This reduction in available calcium then stimulates the cow's own calcium mobilization mechanisms, effectively "training" her system for the post-calving demand.

Mechanism:

Binds calcium in the gut, making it less available for absorption. This triggers the cow's homeostatic mechanisms (parathyroid hormone) to mobilize calcium from bone reserves.

Feeding Duration:

Typically fed for **10-14 days** prior to calving.

Effectiveness:

Studies show it can reduce the incidence of clinical milk fever by **80%** and subclinical hypocalcemia by **50%**.

Palatability:

Generally highly palatable, making it easier to incorporate into the diet compared to some anionic salts.

NZ Context:

Particularly effective in pasture-based systems where achieving a negative DCAD diet can be challenging due to naturally high potassium levels in grass.

Considerations:

Requires precise timing and dosage. Consult with your nutritionist or veterinarian for specific product recommendations and feeding protocols.

Ketosis Monitoring: Beyond Calcium

**Subclinical ketosis** (SCK) is a common metabolic disorder during early lactation, often occurring when the cow's energy intake cannot meet the demands of milk production, leading to excessive fat mobilization and the accumulation of ketone bodies (e.g., BHBA). SCK can significantly impact milk yield, reproductive performance, and increase the risk of other diseases like displaced abomasum and metritis, even without visible clinical signs.

Blood Testing (BHBA):

The most accurate method. Collect blood samples from a representative group of cows (e.g., 10-15 cows) between 5-15 days in milk. A BHBA concentration of **>1.2 mmol/L** indicates subclinical ketosis. Portable meters (e.g., Precision Xtra) provide rapid on-farm results.

Milk Testing (Ketone Strips):

Less accurate than blood but useful for rapid screening. Dip test strips into milk samples. A positive reaction indicates the presence of ketones. Useful for identifying high-risk cows for further investigation.

Urine Testing (Ketone Strips):

Similar to milk strips, provides a quick qualitative assessment. Best used as a preliminary screening tool. Ensure fresh urine samples are collected.

Clinical Signs of Ketosis:

In severe cases, cows may show reduced appetite (especially for concentrates), decreased milk production, weight loss, dull coat, and sometimes nervous signs (e.g., licking, aggression). Early detection of SCK prevents progression to clinical ketosis.

The Main Event: Calcium Support At Calving - Detailed

At calving, the demand for calcium explodes. Even with perfect pre-calving prep, direct calcium supplementation is crucial to prevent subclinical and clinical milk fever. This section compares the primary options for immediate support.

Oral Calcium Boluses

A convenient and effective method for sustained calcium delivery. Easy to administer and avoids palatability issues.

Key Feature: Sustained Release

Products often combine fast and slow-release calcium sources to maintain blood levels for up to 48 hours.

Protocol

Typically 1-2 boluses given as soon as possible after calving, with a possible second dose 12-15 hours later.

Calcium Payload

High. A single dose can provide 40-50g of calcium, often 3x more than an injectable bottle.

Best For

All cows, but especially high-risk animals (older, Jerseys, high BCS). Prophylactic use to prevent milk fever.

Oral Calcium Drenches

A cost-effective liquid option for providing immediate calcium and other nutrients.

Key Feature: Cost-Effective

Often a cheaper alternative to boluses for broad application across the herd.

Protocol

Administered as a single drench at the point of calving.

Considerations

Can be more difficult to administer correctly than a bolus. Risk of aspiration if not done carefully.

Best For

Targeting at-risk animals. Can also support gut motility and has been linked to improved conception rates.

Injectable Calcium (Subcutaneous)

Primarily a treatment for clinical milk fever (down cows), not a preventative tool for the whole herd.

Use Case: Treatment, Not Prevention

Reserved for cows showing clinical signs of milk fever, under veterinary guidance.

Protocol

Warm solution before use. Administer under the skin in at least 5 different spots, massaging each site.

Calcium Payload

Relatively low (~12g per bottle). Provides a rapid spike but is not sustained.

Crucial Follow-Up

Once the cow is sitting up and can swallow, she MUST receive an oral calcium source (bolus or drench) to prevent relapse.

The Recovery: Post-Calving & Colostrum Period

The first few weeks of lactation are a race to close the "energy gap". The cow's intake lags behind her massive energy output for milk production. The goals are to rapidly increase Dry Matter Intake (DMI), maintain rumen health, and produce high-quality colostrum for the calf.

Feeding the Cow: Closing the Energy Gap

The colostrum mob is the highest priority on the farm. They need unlimited access to the best quality pasture, plus strategic supplementation to meet energy demands and prevent acidosis.

Pasture is Key

Provide the highest quality pasture available. Avoid low-fibre, high-nitrogen second-round pasture if possible to reduce acidosis risk.

Strategic Supplementation

Most cows, especially high-producing animals, need 1-2 kg/day of supplement (e.g., silage, concentrates) to balance energy. This is critical for cows calving at lower BCS.

Fibre for Rumen Health

Offer long fibre (hay, straw) to maintain rumen function, especially if feeding high-sugar crops. Rumen buffers or live yeast can also help.

Feeding the Calf: Colostrum is Gold

A healthy cow produces high-quality colostrum, which is vital for calf immunity and lifetime productivity. Success depends on the "3 Qs".

Q1

Quality

Use "gold" (first milking) colostrum. Test with a Brix refractometer if possible (target >22%).

Q2

Quantity

Feed 10-15% of bodyweight (e.g., 4-6 litres for a 40kg calf) within the first 12 hours.

Q3

Quickly

Absorption of antibodies ceases after 24 hours. The first feed within 6 hours is critical.

The Big Picture: Holistic Management & ROI

A successful transition isn't just about feed. It's about a complete system of monitoring, planning, and team execution. Investing in this system provides one of the best returns on investment available in dairy farming.

Trace Elements

The transition period places the highest demand on trace elements like copper, selenium, and zinc. Pre-calving supplementation can boost immune response and reduce mastitis.

Proactive Monitoring

Don't wait for problems. Use blood tests on day 3 calved cows early in the season to check energy and mineral status, allowing for timely adjustments to your plan.

Protocols & Training

Develop clear, simple treatment protocols with your vet. Ensure all staff are trained, equipped, and confident in executing the plan consistently.
