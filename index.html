The Vital 90 Days

An Interactive Protocol for NZ Transition Cow Management

This guide translates the comprehensive transition cow report into an actionable, day-by-day plan. Navigate through the key phases—from 60 days pre-calving to 30 days post-calving—to find targeted advice on nutrition, health, and management to ensure a productive and healthy lactation for your herd.

Phase 1: The Far-Off Dry Period (Days -60 to -21)

This initial phase is foundational. The primary goal is to manage Body Condition Score (BCS) and begin preparing the cow's metabolism for the transition ahead. Management decisions here directly impact her health and productivity weeks later at calving and into early lactation.

Key Goals

  • Achieve target Body Condition Score (BCS).
  • Manage energy intake to avoid over-conditioning.
  • Ensure adequate protein and essential trace elements.
  • Begin foundational mineral supplementation, especially Magnesium.

Nutrition & Feeding

Energy intake is critical. Cows at or below target BCS should receive 100% of ME requirements, while those above target should be restricted to 80-90%. Protein needs are around 12% of the diet, typically met by quality NZ pasture.

Phase 2: The Springer Period (Days -21 to Calving)

This is the most intensive pre-calving phase. The focus shifts dramatically to preparing the cow for the immense calcium demand of calving and lactation. This is when specific strategies to prevent milk fever, like DCAD or Zeolite, are implemented.

Key Goals

  • Prevent clinical and subclinical milk fever (hypocalcemia).
  • Maintain feed intake as calving approaches.
  • Ensure rumen adaptation for the post-calving diet.
  • Continue robust Magnesium supplementation (0.4% of DM).

Nutrition & Feeding

Implement your chosen calcium balance strategy. Keep dietary calcium low (unless using a full DCAD program) to stimulate the cow's own mobilization systems. Avoid high-potassium forages. Monitor urine pH if using a DCAD diet.

Phase 3: The Fresh Cow Period (Days 0 to +30)

The cow has calved and is now in a state of negative energy balance. The absolute priority is to maximize Dry Matter Intake (DMI) to support rapidly increasing milk production and minimize health issues. Nutritional support must be aggressive and precise.

Key Goals

  • Maximize Dry Matter Intake (DMI) with high-quality feed.
  • Provide substantial Calcium supplementation immediately.
  • Manage negative energy balance to prevent ketosis.
  • Support rumen health with adequate effective fibre.

Nutrition & Feeding

Fresh cows are the top priority for feed. Provide unrestricted access to the best pasture and supplements. Supplement with 100-300g limeflour/cow/day. Transition to a positive DCAD diet to stimulate appetite. Ensure adequate protein and carbohydrates for milk production.

Body Condition Score (BCS) Management

Optimal BCS at calving is the single biggest determinant of a successful transition. The goal is not just to hit the average, but to minimize the variation across the herd. Management to achieve these targets must begin in late lactation.

Calcium Balance: DCAD vs. Synthetic Zeolite

Preventing milk fever is critical. Both negative DCAD diets and Synthetic Zeolite are effective strategies, but they work differently and have different implementation requirements. Use this tool to compare them and decide which is best for your system.

How it Works

Induces a mild metabolic acidosis, which "primes" the cow's natural systems to mobilize calcium from bone and increase absorption from the gut. This prepares her for the sudden demand at calving.

Implementation

Requires precise feed analysis, use of low-potassium forages, and anionic salt supplements. Urine pH must be monitored regularly, aiming for a target of 5.5-6.0.

Pros

  • Gold standard, well-researched.
  • Reduces both clinical and subclinical milk fever.
  • Can improve early lactation milk production.

Cons

  • Challenging and costly in NZ pasture systems due to high potassium.
  • High implementation complexity.
  • Potential for palatability issues.

How it Works

Binds to dietary calcium and phosphorus in the rumen, preventing their absorption. This temporary deficiency stimulates the cow to upregulate her own calcium mobilization systems in preparation for calving.

Implementation

Simpler to implement. Fed at 500g/cow/day for 2-3 weeks pre-calving. Must be stopped immediately at calving. Requires ongoing magnesium supplementation. No urine pH testing needed.

Pros

  • Effective in NZ pasture systems.
  • Simple to use, less sensitive to pasture potassium.
  • Proven by DairyNZ trials.

Cons

  • Not recommended with low-phosphorus diets (e.g., fodder beet).
  • Must ensure adequate magnesium levels.
  • Only specific synthetic forms are effective.

Treatment: IV or subcutaneous calcium, supportive care.

Symptoms: Reduced DMI, drop in milk, lethargy, sweet-smelling breath. Caused by severe negative energy balance.

Prevention: Calve cows at optimal BCS (not over-conditioned), maximize DMI post-calving, ensure adequate energy intake.

Treatment: Oral glucose precursors (e.g., propylene glycol), IV glucose in severe cases.

Symptoms: Visible membranes >24hrs post-calving (RFM). Foul-smelling discharge, fever, depression (Metritis).

Prevention: Good transition nutrition (especially calcium), hygiene at calving, minimize calving difficulties, maintain optimal BCS.

Treatment: Supportive care for RFM (do not manually remove). Systemic antibiotics and anti-inflammatories for metritis.

Symptoms: Nervousness, stiff staggering walk, muscle tremors, seizures, sudden death. Caused by magnesium deficiency.

Prevention: Consistent magnesium supplementation pre- and post-calving (20g/day pre, 15-20g/day post). Be aware of high potassium pastures reducing Mg absorption.

Treatment: Immediate veterinary assistance for magnesium therapy.

Technology Integration

Wearable technology is transforming proactive health management. These devices provide real-time data on rumination and eating behavior, allowing for early detection of health issues before clinical signs appear.

Proactive Health Monitoring

A drop in rumination relative to a cow's own baseline is a powerful early indicator of potential problems like ketosis, metritis, or lameness. This allows for early intervention, reducing the severity and cost of disease.

Key Benefits:

  • Early disease detection.
  • Automated heat detection.
  • Reduced on-farm workload.
  • Data-driven management decisions.
